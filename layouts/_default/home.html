01 — About

Fixing the data that science can't use

I'm a two-time Google Summer of Code contributor and two-time Stanford Code in Place instructor who builds research infrastructure for scientists working with data that no existing tool can handle.

The problem I'm focused on right now: what happens when a government agency spends 75 years collecting forest-damage records, and none of it can be used because the coordinates are wrong? That's the scientific problem I'm solving at the University of Florida's Weecology Lab, recovering the U.S. Forest Service's Aerial Detection Survey — decades of ecological history, made usable again.

My move into research wasn't planned, but it wasn't random either. The more I worked with ecological data, the more I saw that the bottleneck is almost never the algorithm — it's the data itself: coordinates that drift across hardware generations, records in incompatible formats, time series with gaps large enough to break the models that now define what questions we can even ask. The people who can fix this are rare. That's the gap I want to work in.

What I'm looking for

A fully funded U.S. graduate position for Spring or Fall 2027 — a Research, Teaching, or Graduate Assistantship. My core is machine learning and computer vision; my deepest current work is in remote sensing and computational ecology, but the methods travel, and I'm glad to apply them in other domains. I'm open to a PhD; what matters most to me is serious, publishable research alongside people who know which questions are worth asking.

Recovering Historical Image Data via Automated Ortho-Registration

Weecology Lab, University of Florida · NumFOCUS / DeepForest

May 2026 — Present
Google Summer of Code 2026 · 4.88% acceptance First-author publication in progress · with UF mentors
  • Selected for GSoC 2026 to work with my mentors Dr. Ethan White (PI), Ben Weinstein & Josh Veitch-Michaelis (research scientists), and Henry Senyondo (research engineer) on a largely unsolved remote-sensing problem.
  • Built a pipeline to align U.S. Forest Service Aerial Detection Survey polygons with year-matched NAIP satellite imagery, correcting 50–500 m spatial offsets caused by GPS imprecision and flight geometry.
  • Designed a hybrid alignment algorithm (inside–outside contrast, contour-to-boundary distance, out-of-bounds penalty) that improved mean IoU from 0.082 → 0.231 on seed pairs.
  • Scaling to ~48,000 Oregon ADS polygons and fine-tuning DeepForest for NAIP-based forest-health detection — and preparing a first-author publication jointly with my University of Florida mentors, releasing the method as a reproducible recipe for long-running survey datasets worldwide.
  • Ran a DeepForest vs. DeepTrees benchmarking study (RetinaNet vs. U-Net + watershed) and presented findings to mentors during selection.
